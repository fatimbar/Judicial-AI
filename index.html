What is MCDA?

Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) is a structured methodology for evaluating complex decisions across multiple competing criteria. In this framework, it is applied to assess judicial AI systems across 9 criteria and 23 sub-indicators, providing a rigorous yet transparent way to compare readiness across jurisdictions.

Unlike single-metric evaluations, MCDA explicitly acknowledges trade-offs — for example, between efficiency gains and rights protection — and uses weighted aggregation to produce a composite score that reflects different value systems.

The Three Weighting Scenarios

Rather than imposing a single set of weights, this framework presents three scenarios to test robustness and surface the political nature of weighting choices.

Scenario A — Equal Weights

All 9 criteria are weighted equally (≈11.1% each).

Justification: Follows the CEPEJ (2022) principle of balanced evaluation across efficiency, rights, and accountability dimensions, ensuring no single value dominates assessment. Equal weighting is the standard MCDA baseline that avoids subjective bias (Belton & Stewart, 2002).

Scenario B — Rights-Prioritising

Criteria related to fundamental rights, transparency, and accountability receive higher weights.

Justification: Aligned with EU AI Act (2024) Article 9 risk management requirements and Recital 47 on high-risk AI systems in justice. Reflects Pasquale's (2015) Black Box Society critique of opacity in automated decision-making, and anchors the framework in ECHR Article 6 and ICCPR Article 14 fair trial guarantees.

Scenario C — Effectiveness-Prioritising

Criteria related to efficiency, accuracy, and implementation receive higher weights.

Justification: Grounded in Rogers's (2003) Diffusion of Innovations theory and CEPEJ (2018) guidelines on efficiency in judicial systems. Reflects a pragmatic adoption perspective in which AI is justified only if it demonstrably improves justice delivery (Pawson & Tilley, 1997; Fixsen et al., 2005).

The 9 Criteria

1. Political Will & Institutional Readiness — Assesses political support, strategy, executive–judicial alignment, urgency, and budget commitment to judicial AI transformation.
2. Legal & Regulatory Framework — Examines the scope of legislation, risk classification, oversight mechanisms, and accreditation standards for judicial AI.
3. Fundamental Rights of Litigants Protection — Evaluates fair trial guarantees, privacy and anti-bias safeguards, and human supervision over AI-assisted decisions.
4. Transparency — Measures technical disclosure, documentation, and public/research access to AI systems used in courts.
5. Technical Maturity — Assesses technological sophistication, reliability, and integration quality of AI systems deployed in the judicial domain.
6. Implementation — Examines operational status, scope of deployment, scaling plans, and availability of usage data.
7. Training & Capacity Building — Evaluates the scope of training programs, resources, and certification for judicial staff working with AI.
8. Actual Impact — Measures documented efficiency gains, productivity improvements, and user/stakeholder satisfaction outcomes.
9. Equality & Justice — Assesses access-widening design, stakeholder engagement in AI development, and bias monitoring with equity outcomes.

Philosophical Framework

  • Critical Realism (Bhaskar) — Recognises stratified reality: empirical observations, actual events, and underlying causal structures in judicial AI adoption.
  • Legal Realism — Treats law as what courts and officials actually do, not only what statutes declare.
  • Feminist Epistemology (Harding, Haraway, Collins) — Standpoint theory and situated knowledges foreground how AI systems encode particular (often gendered, racialised) perspectives.
  • Nested Analysis (Lieberman 2005) — Combines large-N statistical analysis with in-depth case studies to cross-validate findings.

Title

Evaluating Judicial AI Readiness: A Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis Framework Across Legal Traditions

Institution

Stage

First-year PhD (commenced 2025)

Supervisors

[to be updated]

Abstract

This research develops an MCDA framework comprising 9 criteria and 23 sub-indicators to evaluate the readiness of judicial systems to implement artificial intelligence. Through a comparative Global North/South lens, the framework is applied to three case studies: Singapore (Harvey AI), the United States (COMPAS), and Argentina (AymurAI). Drawing on Critical Realism, Legal Realism, and feminist epistemology, the research interrogates how judicial AI systems reproduce or challenge epistemic hierarchies across legal traditions.

Case Studies
